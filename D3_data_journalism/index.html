Observations on the Relationship Between Poverty and Obesity

It has long been theorized that poverty begets obesity. This assumption derives primarily from two generally accepted facts: the most economical meals tend to have poor nutritional value, and those in poverty have less access to health care.

In competitive markets such as food, often the primary methods to increase profitability are to reduce cost and increase quantity supplied. Cutting costs may include using "filler" ingredients, and increasing quantities usually involve speeding the growth/production process using chemicals. The common thread is these all sacrifice the taste & nutritional quality of the food, with the primary recipient being the poorest consumers.

In the American capitalistic environment, individuals are largely left to nagivate the health care system on their own. Those with less means often have low-quality or no plans. In the event of an injury without treatment an individual is more susceptible to developing obesity, and preventative health care is largely absent from these plans.
